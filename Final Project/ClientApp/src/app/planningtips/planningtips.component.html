Planning A Trip

1. Get Your Budget Set.

Entrance Fees

  • Most national parks (about 75%) are free.

  • The rest charge a very reasonable entrance fee, which may even be good for the entire week. The latter averages at about $35 per vehicle.

Foodstuff

  • The lack of restaurants and other ‘food infrastructure’ across National Parks is a blessing and a curse: It’s great not having to get pigeon-holed into tourist traps for sustenance; yet, you’ll have to step up your pre-planning that much more.

  • The Rocket Mortgage team recommends a couple dozen Clif bars and trail mix to start. Read more about camp cooking here. You can likely get away with spending less than $100 for the entire week.

Lodging

  • A hotel can make a big dent in your budget. They are often located outside of parks, which translates to longer drivetimes as well as additional gas costs.

  • Even if you opt for a tent, you will still incur costs. Some sites at the Grand Canyon charge up to $60/night for a campspot.

  • Another option is car-camping: Go all out and build a bed platform in the ol' Subaru!

Backpackers Beware!

  • As freeing as backpacking can be, it’s not always cheap. Just visit your local REI or Moosejaw store and you’ll know what we mean.

  • Simply outfitting yourself with a lightweight gear (and by mile 10 you’ll realize the value-add of lightweight gear) like a backpack, sleeping bag and tent, you will likely end up spending over $500.

  • Add to that a suite of protective clothing items, cooking infrastructure, and other navigation accessories, you may be lucky to keep it under $1,500.

  • ...but you may end up keeping and reusing this gear for decades.

2. Organize and Pack Your Gear
  • Bring water and good boots.

  • Everything else is optional.

It doesn’t take long to go off the rails on gear prep. Remain calm and stoic, and refrain from reading adventure novels before visiting your local Moosejaw or REI Co-op.



3. Plan the Drive.
  • Even within the same park, scenic destinations can be hours apart.

  • Unless you’ve got the Dutch biking gene and an unparalleled grit to navigate rugged rural infrastructure, plan to arrive in a motorized vehicle.

  • GoogleMaps or MyMaps (also a Google product) are great tools to plan trips with stops. Click here for more details.

4. Stay on the Trail!

Ever heard of 'Death Valley?'


  • The national parks have thousands of miles of marked hiking trails. Tip: Follow them.

The idea of ‘Stay on the Trail’ applies to the realms of safety and preservation:


  • Leave No Trace – more important than removing a stray rock or two, do not leave any trash or litter behind.

  • Don’t Feed Wildlife – feeding wild animals isn’t a good idea and is prohibited in national parks. It can also make wild animals lose their natural fear of people, compromising the safety of both the animal and humans.

  • No Smoking – though you might find space enough to prevent creating second-hand smoke, smoking is a fire hazard that is all too preventable. Not a good look given the past couple years of bad wildfires, many of which have sadly been caused by humans.
